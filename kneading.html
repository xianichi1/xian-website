Kneading

Kneading—also known as “making biscuits”—is when cats press their paws into soft surfaces, often while purring. It starts in kittenhood as a nursing instinct and continues into adulthood as a comforting behavior.

  • Instinct from kittenhood: Kittens knead to stimulate milk from their mother and carry this soothing behavior into adulthood.
  • Marking territory: Scent glands in their paws allow cats to leave their mark while kneading.
  • Comfort and bonding: Many cats knead when they're relaxed or feel safe.
  • Affection: Kneading on their humans is a strong signal of trust and love.

Source: AAHA

Zoomies

Zoomies, officially called Frenetic Random Activity Periods (FRAPs), are sudden bursts of energy where cats dart around seemingly out of nowhere.

  • After using the litter box: Some cats sprint away instinctively after going potty.
  • Releasing built-up energy: Cats nap a lot, and zoomies help burn excess energy.
  • Excitement or stimulation: Playtime or seeing their owner can trigger it.
  • Natural instincts: Zoomies mimic the thrill of the chase or a mock hunt.

Source: Caring Hearts Animal Hospital

Purring

Purring is a vibration created by the rapid movement of a cat’s laryngeal muscles. Though it’s commonly linked to happiness, it serves several other emotional and physiological purposes too.

  • Happiness: A relaxed cat often purrs while being petted or napping.
  • Self-soothing: Cats may purr during stressful times, like vet visits or injury.
  • Healing: Some researchers suggest purring may aid in recovery due to its low-frequency vibration.
  • Communication: Cats might purr to bond with humans or signal they’re not a threat.

Source: PetMD

Chasing

Chasing is deeply tied to a cat’s hunting instinct, and even indoor cats exhibit this behavior regularly with toys, other pets, or even humans.

  • Play behavior: Cats chase for fun and to mimic stalking prey.
  • Boredom: A lack of stimulation can lead to chasing feet, tails, or shadows.
  • Stress or energy outlet: Cats might chase when they’re overstimulated or anxious.
  • Curiosity: Cats are naturally curious and may chase moving objects to investigate.

Source: DeziRoo
